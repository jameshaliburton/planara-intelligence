Competitive Landscape at the Knowledge Layer

Where Planara sits in the manufacturing intelligence ecosystem — direct competitors, adjacent players, and the white space nobody owns.

Planara Manufacturing Intelligence · March 2026 · Confidential
The Thesis

There are two layers of intelligence in manufacturing. One is well-served. The other isn't.

The machine data layer — sensor telemetry, predictive maintenance, OEE optimization — is a crowded market. MicroAI, Sight Machine, Uptake, Augury, and the edge computing ecosystem have this covered. The big platform players (PTC ThingWorx, Siemens MindSphere, GE Vernova) play here too.

The knowledge layer — documentation intelligence, service knowledge, dealer enablement, owner self-service — is fragmented, underserved, and largely pre-AI. This is where Planara operates.

Several companies touch parts of this layer. Nobody connects it end-to-end across the full value chain: OEM → Dealer → Technician → Owner.

Planara is the intelligence layer. Not a platform that replaces existing tools — the layer that makes them smart. We ingest manufacturer documentation and telemetry, produce structured role-specific intelligence, and surface it wherever it's needed: in our own purpose-built interfaces, inside a DMS, alongside an owner app, or via API into any other system.

Why This Matters

This isn't about intelligent manuals. It's about aftermarket revenue capture.

Nobody buys a $350K boat because of a better manual. The purchase decision is already made. The question that matters is: once someone owns complex equipment, how does intelligence create value for three parties?

The revenue mechanics

Today's aftermarket revenue capture chain is leaky. An owner notices a problem → calls the dealer (maybe) → dealer diagnoses from scratch → orders parts (maybe the right ones) → schedules return visit (maybe). At every step, friction causes drop-off. Owners defer service. Dealers miss opportunities. The OEM loses aftermarket revenue to inaction, independent shops, or third-party parts.

An intelligence layer tightens every link in that chain:

Friction point (today) With intelligence layer Revenue impact
Owner doesn't know service is due until something breaks Proactive, telemetry-aware alerts: "847 hours, major service due at 1000. Newport Marine has availability." Preventive service appointments that would have been missed. Earlier intervention = simpler, higher-margin work.
Owner calls dealer, describes problem poorly. Dealer guesses. Owner interacts with intelligence first. Dealer receives pre-diagnosed context with telemetry data and probable cause. Higher first-time fix rate. Fewer return visits. Right parts on first trip. More billable hours per appointment.
Owner doesn't know which parts to order or whether they're OEM-genuine. Intelligence surfaces exact OEM part numbers, SKUs, prices, and stock status. "Order from your dealer" button. OEM parts revenue captured instead of lost to aftermarket/third-party. Parts attached to every service interaction.
Owner relationship with dealer goes dormant between service visits. Dealer-branded portal maintains year-round engagement: seasonal guidance, equipment tips, usage insights. Dealer retention. Owner comes back to Newport Marine instead of shopping around. Lifetime customer value increases.
OEM has no visibility into owner behavior or field conditions. Every query, alert, and service event flows back as structured data. Pattern detection across fleet. Product improvement signals. Documentation gap identification. Warranty cost reduction through earlier intervention.

There's also a secondary value that hasn't been explored yet: verified equipment history as a resale asset. A complete, AI-documented service and condition history — not just "oil changed on date X" but structured diagnostic records, telemetry trends, and resolution documentation — is a differentiator at resale. It's Carfax for complex equipment, generated as a natural byproduct of the intelligence layer doing its job. This benefits the owner (higher resale price), the dealer (trade-in confidence), and the OEM (brand value tied to documented longevity).

The intelligence layer isn't the product. Aftermarket revenue capture is the product. The intelligence layer is the mechanism. Every proactive alert is a service appointment. Every diagnostic answer is a parts order. Every ongoing engagement is dealer retention. Documentation is the raw material — revenue is the output.

The Owner Experience

Nobody is designing for the equipment owner

Every player in this landscape is building for professionals — technicians, service managers, factory operators. The equipment owner (the person who bought the $350K boat, the fleet of commercial vehicles, the industrial compressor) is an afterthought. They get a PDF manual, a phone number, and maybe a maintenance log app.

This matters because the owner is the revenue engine of the aftermarket. Every owner question is a potential service appointment, parts order, or dealer touchpoint. The companies that figure out how to serve the owner intelligently will capture the largest share of aftermarket revenue.

What the current owner experience looks like

A Yamaha F300 owner at 6am on the water with a warning light has three options: call their dealer (who opens at 7:30), Google it (and find forum posts from 2018), or ignore it until the next scheduled service. None of these are good. The owner has a $350K asset and no intelligent way to understand what's happening with it in the moment.

What we need to get right

The demo on the Planara site shows a conversational query interface — the owner types a question and gets a structured answer. That's a good proof of concept for showing Yamaha's internal team what's possible. But the actual shipped owner product probably looks different:

Interaction model What it means When it's right
Push-based (proactive) The system tells the owner what matters before they ask. Smart notifications: "Your engine has 847 hours. 1000-hour major service due. Newport Marine has availability next week." → One tap to schedule. Most of the time. Owners don't know what to ask. The system should anticipate needs based on telemetry, service history, and seasonal context. This is where most of the service revenue capture happens.
Reactive (query-based) The owner asks a question and gets a structured answer. "What does this warning light mean?" → Simplified diagnostic with clear next steps and a "Call your dealer" button. When something goes wrong. Warning lights, unusual behavior, unexpected situations. The answers should be simplified for an owner, not the dense procedural output a technician gets.
Seasonal / lifecycle Contextual guidance tied to calendar and equipment lifecycle. Winterization checklists in October. Pre-season checks in March. Warranty expiration reminders. Resale value documentation. Ongoing relationship maintenance. This is what keeps the owner engaged with their dealer year-round, not just when something breaks.

The key insight: the technician tool is query-first because technicians know what to ask. The owner tool should be push-first — proactive, contextual, action-oriented. The intelligence layer is the same. The interface and interaction model are different.

Rollout Strategy

What we build, in what order, and why

Phase 1 — Prove the Intelligence Layer

What we sell: Documentation intelligence. We ingest a manufacturer's manuals and produce structured, citation-backed, role-specific output that's demonstrably better than what generic AI or existing tools can do.

How we prove it: Custom-built demo interfaces (the service tool, the owner portal). These aren't the product — they're the proof that the intelligence layer works. A manufacturer sees their F300 documentation returning real specs, real part numbers, real safety warnings.

What we don't do yet: We don't try to replace anyone's DMS. We don't build fleet analytics. We don't require telemetry integration. We keep the surface area small so we can execute flawlessly.

Technical moat to build: Domain-level documentation ingestion quality. If our RAG pipeline produces consistently better structured output from marine/powersports manuals than anything else on the market — that's what makes the demo land and the pilot succeed. This isn't visible on a marketing page but it's the thing that wins.

Phase 2 — Expand into Telemetry + Integrations

What we add: Live telemetry connection (Siren Marine or equivalent). DMS integration (Lightspeed, CDK) for work orders and parts ordering. These make the intelligence contextual and actionable — not just "what oil does it need" but "your engine has 847 hours, major service is due in 153 hours, here are the parts, here's your closest dealer."

Why now: The pilot has proven the documentation intelligence works. Now we add the data connections that turn answers into actions. This is where Planara becomes hard to rip out — once we're connected to telemetry and DMS, we're infrastructure.

Competitive defense: We're now integrated with the same DMS systems that the incumbents depend on. We're a complement, not a threat. A DMS provider is more likely to partner with us than build what we do.

Phase 3 — The Dealer/Owner Experience (The Strategic Moat)

What we build: The white-label dealer portal infrastructure. Each dealer gets their own branded intelligence interface for their customers. This is the thing that nobody else is building and that the incumbents are structurally unable to prioritize.

Critical design insight: The owner experience should be push-first, not query-first. Technicians know what to ask. Owners don't. The shipped product should lead with proactive, telemetry-aware notifications ("847 hours — major service due, your dealer has availability") and seasonal guidance ("winterization checklist for your F300"), with the query interface as a secondary mode for when something goes wrong. The demo on the site shows query-based interaction because it demonstrates the intelligence layer. The real product inverts the model: the system tells the owner what matters, the owner acts on it.

Why this is the moat: Neuron7 sells to Fortune 1000 VP of Service. ServiceMax sells to enterprise IT. Nobody is selling to the dealer network — because it requires a completely different deployment model (per-dealer white-labeling), a different buyer (OEM's dealer relations team), a different UX (consumer-grade, proactive, mobile-first), and a fundamentally different interaction model (push not pull). Building this capability is what makes Planara defensible, not just fast.

Revenue model: Per-dealer SaaS, paid by the OEM as a value-add for their dealer network. Every dealer portal is a recurring revenue line. Every owner interaction is a measurable service revenue event for the dealer. The OEM pays because it drives dealer loyalty and aftermarket capture.

Phase 4 — OEM Intelligence (The Flywheel)

What emerges: At scale, the data flowing through the platform reveals patterns: which documentation sections generate the most queries, which failure modes are most common across model lines, which dealers are seeing unusual service patterns. This is fleet intelligence for the OEM product and documentation teams.

Why last: This requires volume. You can't do fleet analytics with one pilot. But it's the thing you sell from day one as a vision — because it's what makes a C-suite buyer care. "Your documentation investment becomes an intelligence asset that improves your products" is a boardroom story.

The expansion logic

Powersports is the immediate adjacent play. It's not really a "new vertical" — it's the same ecosystem. Yamaha makes outboards AND motorcycles, ATVs, snowmobiles, and PWC. BRP makes boats AND Ski-Doo, Can-Am, Sea-Doo. Honda, Kawasaki, Polaris span both. Lightspeed DMS serves both marine and powersports dealers. A Yamaha marine pilot extends naturally to Yamaha powersports with minimal platform adaptation. The $12B+ powersports aftermarket runs through the same dealer channel with the same service dynamics.

RV/Trailer is the second move. High-value assets with the most complex documentation of any consumer equipment (a motorhome is a house on wheels — chassis, engine, electrical, plumbing, HVAC, appliances, all from different manufacturers). Thor Industries ($10B+), Winnebago, REV Group have dealer networks. Lightspeed DMS serves RV dealers. Owners are extremely engaged (the RV community is one of the most active enthusiast groups in North America). And the aftermarket is massive — parts, service, upgrades, winterization, storage.

Commercial HVAC is the medium-term opportunity with the strongest aftermarket economics. Carrier, Trane, and Lennox have dealer/contractor networks. The technician shortage is projected at 300,000+ by 2031. Service contracts are the dominant revenue model. But the "owner" is a facility manager, not a consumer — different UX needs, different buying process. It's a natural fit for the intelligence layer but requires adapting the interface for a B2B context.

Construction and medical devices are someone else's fight. Elevat owns the construction/heavy equipment space with their Bosch partnership. Neuron7 owns medical devices with Medtronic. Both have a head start, established relationships, and industry-specific expertise. We should avoid direct competition here unless an opportunity falls into our lap.

The expansion path is: Marine (now) → Powersports (same OEMs, same DMS, minimal adaptation) → RV/Trailer (same DMS, similar owner profile, high documentation complexity) → Commercial HVAC (different owner type but strongest aftermarket economics). Each step builds on the previous one's infrastructure, relationships, and learnings.

Summary

Planara's position — honestly

We're not a platform with three products. We're an intelligence layer that produces role-specific output from manufacturer documentation and telemetry. The custom interfaces we build (service tool, owner portal) are proof points, not the product. The product is the intelligence itself — and it can surface in our own UIs, inside a DMS, alongside an owner app like MyYamahaOutboards, or via API into any other system.

The competitive landscape is more crowded and faster-moving than it was a month ago. Elevat's Feb 2026 launch with Bosch/HydraForce shows that the "OEM-branded AI for dealer networks" thesis is being validated by the market — and we're not the only ones who see it. Neuron7's $44M raise shows enterprise service AI has investor confidence. The window exists but it's not wide open.

Our path: prove the intelligence layer with Yamaha marine. Expand to powersports (same OEMs, same DMS). Build the owner experience moat that nobody else — not Elevat, not Neuron7, not ServiceMax — is building. Then use that position to expand into RV/trailer and eventually commercial HVAC. Each step makes the next one easier and the moat deeper.

The moat isn't the RAG pipeline — that's commodity infrastructure. The moat is the combination of domain-level documentation intelligence quality (Phase 1), telemetry and DMS integration depth (Phase 2), the proactive push-based owner experience nobody else is building (Phase 3), and a vertical expansion path through adjacent industries that share infrastructure (marine → powersports → RV → HVAC). Each phase makes the next harder to replicate.
